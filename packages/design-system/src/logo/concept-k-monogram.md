# Concept: The Structural K

- **The idea.** A K read as a hull in cross-section: the stem is the keel blade — a dead-vertical structural spine that over-runs the arms top and bottom, dropping below as a real fin keel does; the two arms are the rising sheer (deck) and the turning bilge (waterline), branching off a single riveted joint.
- **Why it reads "Metador."** The spine carries the load, not the angles — exactly Metador's thesis (the structural member that keeps a vessel upright is the visible backbone, with everything else hung off it); the rivet names the joint as engineered, not decorative.
- **How it survives 16px.** Three straight strokes at a constant 5/64 weight, no curves and no fills to clog; the over-running vertical keeps an unmistakable K silhouette even when the rivet fuses into the spine at favicon scale.
- **Divergence vs Hyperliquid blob.** It is hard, straight, and skeletal — pure naval geometry with zero organic mass, the structural opposite of HL's soft morphing blob/butterfly field.
- **Divergence vs Sui drop.** No droplet, no curve, no enclosed fluid form; this is a rigid spar drawn in brass-line, the antithesis of a rounded water-drop.
